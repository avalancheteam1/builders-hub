## Introducing Avalanche Helicon

Helicon is the next upgrade to the Avalanche Network, activating first on the Fuji Testnet ahead of its Mainnet activation. In line with recent upgrades, Octane and Granite, Helicon continues to improve and scale the C-Chain, introducing Streaming Asynchronous Execution (SAE). It also adds a series of defining changes regarding the network's staking economics.

The upgrade is driven by five Avalanche Community Proposals:

- [ACP-194: Streaming Asynchronous Execution](/docs/acps/194-streaming-asynchronous-execution)
- [ACP-236: Auto-Renewed Staking](/docs/acps/236-auto-renewed-staking)
- [ACP-273: Reduce Minimum Staking Duration](https://github.com/avalanche-foundation/ACPs/tree/main/ACPs/273-reduce-minimum-staking-duration)
- [ACP-283: Dynamic Minimum Gas Price](https://github.com/avalanche-foundation/ACPs/tree/main/ACPs/283-dynamic-minimum-gas-price)
- [ACP-285: Reduce Minimum Consumption Rate](https://github.com/avalanche-foundation/ACPs/tree/main/ACPs/285-reduce-minimum-consumption-rate) (progressive rollout post-Helicon activation)

A note on timing: Helicon activates on Fuji Testnet this Monday, July 20, 2026; the required AvalancheGo version will be announced with the Fuji Testnet pre-release. Mainnet activation will be announced separately.

## Institutional Staking & Enhanced Economic Model

Helicon reshapes how validators commit capital to the primary network with ACP-236 and ACP-273, then adjusts the economics behind those changes with ACP-285, which rolls in progressively after activation rather than all at once. These staking changes apply to the primary network (P-Chain) only and do not change how validators operate on individual Avalanche L1s.

### Auto-Renewed Staking (ACP-236)

<Callout type="info">
Operators running multiple nodes spend significant time and resources manually re-staking at the end of every period.

**ACP-236 eliminates that overhead entirely.**
</Callout>

Managing validator lifecycle at scale means recurring key-signing overhead and reward gaps at every period boundary. ACP-236 makes staking a set-and-forget treasury operation: an operator stakes once and keeps validating, rewards compound on their own, and key signing drops to onboarding and the occasional deliberate config change. Validators stop churning out of the set at every period boundary, which helps network stability as much as it helps the operator.

ACP-236 replaces the fixed end time with a cycle duration (`period`) and an auto-compound ratio, implemented through three new transaction types:

- `AddAutoRenewedValidatorTx` registers the validator with a cycle duration and auto-compound ratio instead of an end date.
- `RewardAutoRenewedValidatorTx` is issued automatically by the block builder at each cycle's end. It is consensus-issued, with nothing for the operator to call.
- `SetAutoRenewedValidatorConfigTx` updates the `period` and auto-compound ratio for the next cycle. It must be signed by the validator's configured owner, and changes take effect only at the next cycle boundary.

At each cycle's end, the outcome follows from uptime and reward eligibility. With sufficient uptime, rewards are split by the `autoCompoundRewardShares` ratio (the set portion restaked, the rest withdrawn) and a new cycle begins immediately. The operator sets that ratio in parts per million: 1,000,000 restakes the full reward, 300,000 restakes 30% and pays out the rest, and 0 pays out entirely. Otherwise the validator exits and its principal and previously accrued rewards are returned, with the current cycle's reward forfeited when uptime fell short. Config changes take effect only at the next cycle boundary, and setting the next `period` to 0 is the deliberate exit signal.

Delegations do not auto-renew. Only the validator's own stake renews, and a delegation must fit inside a single cycle, since the validator's continuation beyond the current cycle isn't guaranteed.

### Shorter Minimum Staking Durations (ACP-273)

<Callout type="info">
Institutional allocators often can't commit capital for weeks at a time.

**ACP-273 brings the minimum staking window inside the redemption timelines they actually operate under.**
</Callout>

Minimum staking duration currently sits at two weeks, and that creates friction with institutional requirements (a regulated allocator has to be able to return funds within 48 hours).

Fixing this was the motivation behind ACP-273. Reducing the primary-network minimum validation period from 336 hours (two weeks) to 48 hours (two days) keeps validators inside the redemption window without weakening the security guarantees for the network.

Working alongside ACP-236, the shorter minimum is more than a smaller number. Short auto-renewing cycles let an operator stay continuously validating while keeping each individual commitment brief, which is continuity and capital flexibility at once.

### Staking Reward Curve (ACP-285) 

<Callout type="info">
Making short-term staking more accessible is only half of the equation.

**ACP-285 ensures the reward curve still meaningfully incentivizes longer commitments.**
</Callout>

ACP-236 and ACP-273 both make short-term staking more attractive, which widens a problem the reward curve already had: the premium for staking longer has been flattening. A two-week stake now earns roughly 84 to 85% of the one-year rate, and the spread between the shortest and longest durations has fallen from about 1.9 percentage points at Genesis to around 1 point today. ACP-285 restores that premium by adjusting the curve.

It reduces annual AVAX issuance by lowering the mean consumption rate from 10% to 7.5%. The one-year rate at the top of the curve is unchanged; shorter durations earn less, which steepens the curve rather than shifting it down. Because the maximum consumption rate is untouched, the spread between the shortest and longest stakes widens from about 1 percentage point toward 2.3. A structural model of validator and delegator behavior projects the stake-weighted average staking duration could rise and annual issuance could fall by roughly 0.5 to 1 percentage point, stretching the security-budget runway against the 720M supply cap, though the behavioral response is uncertain and won't be confirmed until post-activation data. 

ACP-285 ramps in over roughly 90 days from Helicon's activation, starting at 10% and settling at 7.5% along a straight line, so protocols and strategies priced on current rates can reprice instead of absorbing a discontinuous shock.

<Callout type="info" title="Note">
A 2.5-point cut to the mean consumption rate is not a 2.5-point cut to yield, because the remaining-supply-ratio multiplier means the shortest-duration rate drops only about 1.3 points.
</Callout>

## C-Chain Improvements 

Helicon's other two proposals sit on the C-Chain. The headline change is ACP-194, Streaming Asynchronous Execution, which re-architects how the chain executes blocks for higher throughput. ACP-283 rides alongside it, replacing the C-Chain's minimum gas price with a real, governable floor. 

### Streaming Asynchronous Execution (ACP-194)

<Callout type="info">
The C-Chain's current architecture forces consensus and execution to take turns.

**ACP-194 runs them in parallel, removing the bottleneck.**
</Callout>

Streaming Asynchronous Execution is the headline change in Helicon. Today the C-Chain executes a block's transactions before consensus can move on, so consensus and execution take turns and throughput is capped by that back-and-forth.

<HeliconSaeDiagram diagram="parallel-streams" />

ACP-194 decouples the two: consensus accepts blocks into a queue while a separate executor works through them in parallel, so the chain does more in the same wall-clock time.

<HeliconSaeDiagram diagram="block-relationship" />

One nuance, relevant to a narrow slice of tooling, is that a transaction's effects finalize a moment after its block is accepted rather than at the instant of acceptance. For the full picture, including the interactive transaction-lifecycle walkthrough, the [Streaming Asynchronous Execution](/docs/primary-network/streaming-async-execution) doc is the best resource.

### A Dynamic Minimum Gas Price (ACP-283)

<Callout type="info">
A fixed minimum gas price left the network vulnerable to spam.

**ACP-283 replaces it with a validator-governed floor that can respond to real conditions.**
</Callout>

ACP-176 (Octane) set the C-Chain's minimum gas price at 1 wei, on the assumption that the dynamic fee mechanism would push gas prices up under congestion. In practice the lowest observed C-Chain gas prices have sat around 10^5 wei, cheap enough that spam protocols like XEN stayed profitable. The community's stopgap has been to inflate gas prices with failing transactions, which costs the network several AVAX a day. ACP-283 replaces that stopgap with a real, governable fee floor.

Instead of a fixed floor, the minimum gas price becomes a value validators converge on by stake-weighted vote, the same mechanism already used for dynamic gas targets (ACP-176) and dynamic block times (ACP-226). Each validator can set a `min-price-target` in wei for its preferred floor, or abstain by default and inherit the previous block's setting. Because validators produce blocks in proportion to their stake, the effective floor settles at the stake-weighted median preference. 

Adjustment is bound through the `BlocksToDouble` parameter, specified by this ACP at 3,600. This parameter expresses the number of consecutive maximum-change blocks required to halve or double the floor under sustained voting pressure, taking roughly an hour to double or halve, which leaves the network room to react.

## Technical Changelog

The concrete changes for developers and node operators, ordered by ACP.

- **ACP-194** — transaction effects are final at settlement, shortly after a block is accepted; target settlement timing when reading receipts or state.
- **ACP-236** — new P-Chain transactions `AddAutoRenewedValidatorTx`, `SetAutoRenewedValidatorConfigTx` (owner-signed; `period 0` exits), and `RewardAutoRenewedValidatorTx` (consensus-issued). `platform.getCurrentValidators` embeds an `AutoRenewedConfig` for auto-renewed validators, containing `nextPeriod`, `autoCompoundRewardShares` (ppm), and `validatorAuthority`.
- **ACP-273** — minimum staking duration reduced to 48 hours.
- **ACP-283** — new `min-price-target` (wei) for the validator-voted minimum gas price; fetch fee parameters from the network instead of assuming a fixed floor.
- **ACP-285** — mean consumption rate ramps from 10% to 7.5% over 90 days from activation.
