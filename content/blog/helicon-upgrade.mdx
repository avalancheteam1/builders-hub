## Introducing Avalanche Helicon

Helicon is the next upgrade to the Avalanche Network, currently in testing ahead of its activation on the Fuji Testnet. Where recent upgrades like Octane and Granite focused on C-Chain fees and block timing, Helicon's defining changes sit on the P-Chain and the network's staking economics.

The upgrade is driven by four Avalanche Community Proposals:

- [ACP-236: Auto-Renewed Staking](/docs/acps/236-auto-renewed-staking)
- [ACP-273: Reduce Minimum Staking Duration](https://github.com/avalanche-foundation/ACPs/tree/main/ACPs/273-reduce-minimum-staking-duration)
- [ACP-194: Streaming Asynchronous Execution](/docs/acps/194-streaming-asynchronous-execution)
- [ACP-283: Dynamic Minimum Gas Price](https://github.com/avalanche-foundation/ACPs/tree/main/ACPs/283-dynamic-minimum-gas-price)

A note on timing: activation times and the required AvalancheGo version will be announced with the Fuji Testnet pre-release. This post covers what each change does and why, not when it activates.

---

## Why Staking Is the Center of Gravity

Most network upgrades ship performance. Helicon ships performance too, but three of its four proposals exist to answer a concrete operational or economic requirement, not just to make the network faster.

Two of them, ACP-236 and ACP-273, reshape how validators commit capital to the primary network. A third, ACP-283, closes an economic gap that was costing the network real AVAX every day. The fourth, Streaming Asynchronous Execution, is the exception: no business requirement sits behind it, only a change to how the C-Chain executes blocks. Each is covered below, the requirement first where one exists.

---

## Auto-Renewed Staking (ACP-236)

Today a primary-network validator commits to a fixed end time within minimum and maximum duration constraints, and when the period ends the operator has to re-stake by hand. For anyone running more than a handful of nodes, node providers, custodians, and institutional operators especially, that manual model is expensive in three ways. Re-staking every period is labor-intensive and repeats for every node on every cycle. Each re-stake also means signing a transaction, so a position that should be set-and-forget turns into recurring access to staking keys, exactly the surface custodial setups work to minimize. And between one period ending and the next beginning, the stake sits idle and earns nothing.

Helicon's answer is to let an operator stake once and keep validating, with rewards handled automatically and no recurring key signing.

ACP-236 replaces the fixed end time with a cycle duration (`period`) and an auto-compound ratio, implemented through three new transaction types:

- `AddAutoRenewedValidatorTx` registers the validator with a cycle duration and auto-compound ratio instead of an end date.
- `RewardAutoRenewedValidatorTx` is issued automatically by the block builder at each cycle's end. It is consensus-issued, with nothing for the operator to call.
- `SetAutoRenewedValidatorConfigTx` updates the `period` and auto-compound ratio for the next cycle. It must be signed by the validator's configured owner, and changes take effect only at the next cycle boundary.

The auto-compound ratio (`autoCompoundRewardShares`) is set in parts per million: `300000` restakes 30% of each cycle's rewards and pays out the other 70%, while `1000000` compounds the full amount. The restaked portion raises validator weight up to the maximum validator stake, and any overflow is paid out instead.

Uptime is measured separately for each cycle, and the outcome at each cycle end follows from it. With sufficient uptime and `period` greater than zero, rewards are split by the auto-compound ratio and a new cycle begins immediately. With sufficient uptime and `period` set to zero, principal and all pending rewards are returned and the validator exits gracefully, so setting `--period 0` is the exit signal. With insufficient uptime, the validator is force-exited: principal and previously accrued rewards are returned, but the current cycle's reward is forfeited.

Delegations do not auto-renew. Only the validator's own stake renews, and a delegation must fit inside a single cycle, since the validator's continuation beyond the current cycle isn't guaranteed.

The net effect is that staking becomes a set-and-forget treasury operation. Rewards compound on their own, key signing drops to onboarding and the occasional deliberate config change, and validators stop churning out of the set at every period boundary, which helps network stability as much as it helps the operator.

## Shorter Minimum Staking Durations (ACP-273)

The minimum staking duration today is two weeks, and for anyone who needs flexible access to liquidity a 14-day lockup is friction that keeps both capital and validators off the network. ACP-273 lowers that barrier without weakening the security guarantees that hold while a validator is actually staked. It cuts the minimum on the primary network from 336 hours (two weeks) to 48 hours (two days), so a staker can reach their capital in two days instead of fourteen. The maximum duration is unchanged.

Under today's reward curve, the yield given up for a shorter commitment is small, on the order of 0.04 percentage points between a short and a long stake, so the two-week floor was never doing much reward-side work. That balance is set to change. A separate reward-curve adjustment, ACP-285, lowers AVAX emissions and widens the premium paid to longer commitments: the 365-day rate holds where it is today, while shorter durations earn somewhat less and the gap between the shortest and longest stakes grows. ACP-285 is not part of Helicon, and like these changes it is P-Chain only and leaves anything already staked at its current rate. Read together the direction is clear. The network is making short-term staking easy to enter while paying longer commitments more, and letting stakers choose where on that curve they want to sit.

Paired with ACP-236, the shorter minimum is more than a smaller number. Short auto-renewing cycles let an operator stay continuously validating while keeping each individual commitment brief, which is continuity and capital flexibility at once.

The proposal names its own tradeoff. Shorter minimums combined with auto-renewal mean the validator set composition can shift over much shorter windows, which matters for consensus stability and Interchain Messaging delivery. That coupling is why ACP-236 and ACP-273 are evaluated together rather than in isolation.

## A Dynamic Minimum Gas Price (ACP-283)

ACP-176 (Octane) set the C-Chain's minimum gas price at 1 wei, on the assumption that the dynamic fee mechanism would push prices up under congestion. In practice the lowest observed C-Chain gas prices have sat around 10^5 wei, cheap enough that spam protocols like XEN stayed profitable. The community's stopgap has been to inflate gas prices with failing transactions, which costs the network several AVAX a day. ACP-283 replaces that stopgap with a real, governable fee floor.

Instead of a fixed floor, the minimum gas price becomes a value validators converge on by stake-weighted vote, the same mechanism already used for dynamic gas targets (ACP-176) and dynamic block times (ACP-226). Each validator sets a `min-price-target` in wei for its preferred floor, abstaining by default and inheriting the previous block's setting. Because validators produce blocks in proportion to their stake, the effective floor settles at the stake-weighted median preference. Adjustment is bounded, taking roughly an hour to double or halve even under sustained pressure, which leaves the network room to react.

For developers the takeaway is one line: do not hardcode a gas-price floor in C-Chain tooling. Fetch fee parameters from the network.

## Streaming Asynchronous Execution (ACP-194)

Streaming Asynchronous Execution (SAE) is the architectural change in Helicon. Unlike the proposals above it answers no business requirement; it changes how the C-Chain executes blocks, for throughput.

SAE decouples consensus from execution. When validators approve a block it becomes accepted and enters a FIFO execution queue, but its transactions are not final yet. A parallel executor works through the queue and produces state roots, and a block becomes settled once a following block references its execution results, typically a few seconds after acceptance. Splitting the two stages removes the context switching between consensus and execution and lets gas consumption track wall-clock time more closely, which is where the concurrent throughput gains come from. Security holds through worst-case-cost validation, which guarantees an accepted transaction can always pay its eventual execution fee.

For builders the practical point is timing. A transaction's outcome, its receipt and contract effects, is final at settlement, shortly after acceptance rather than at the moment of acceptance. Confirmation logic that assumes effects are readable the instant a block is accepted should account for that gap. For the full mechanism, see [Streaming Asynchronous Execution](/docs/primary-network/streaming-async-execution).

## Summary of Changes

A technical overview of what Helicon introduces, for developers and node operators.

### New P-Chain Transaction Types (ACP-236)

- `AddAutoRenewedValidatorTx` — register an auto-renewed validator (cycle duration + auto-compound ratio).
- `SetAutoRenewedValidatorConfigTx` — owner-signed update to the next cycle's `period` and auto-compound ratio; `period 0` signals graceful exit.
- `RewardAutoRenewedValidatorTx` — consensus-issued at each cycle end; not user-callable.

### New Validator Fields

Auto-renewed validators report additional fields in `platform.getCurrentValidators`:

- `period` — the cycle duration.
- `autoCompoundRewardShares` — auto-compound ratio in parts per million (e.g. `400000` = 40%).
- `validatorAuthority` — the configured config owner.

### Staking Parameters

- Minimum staking duration reduced to 48 hours (ACP-273).

### C-Chain (ACP-283)

- New `min-price-target` configuration value (wei) for validators to vote on the minimum gas price. Tooling should fetch fee parameters from the network rather than assuming a fixed floor.

### C-Chain Execution (ACP-194)

- Transaction effects are final at settlement, shortly after a block is accepted. Confirmation logic that reads receipts or contract state should target settlement timing.
